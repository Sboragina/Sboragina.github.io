1. Type 1 Diabetes:
The Basics

"Diabetes" [cropped] by Bradley Johnson / CC BY-NC-ND 2.0

Objectives

After completing this topic, you will be able to:

  • Differentiate between type 1 diabetes and type 2 diabetes.
  • Use symptoms of and risk factors for type 1 diabetes to determine who may be at risk of developing it.

Introduction

Diabetes is one of the most talked about diseases in North America. Again and again we hear about how obesity and lifestyle choices are major risk factors for developing diabetes — except this isn't quite true — they are major risk factor for developing type 2 diabetes. The causes of other types of diabetes have nothing to do with obesity or lifestyle choices. And these other types of diabetes are rarely mentioned.

In this module, we'll look at Type 1 diabetes. Start by watching this short introduction by actor Victor Garber, who was diagnosed with type 1 diabetes in 1962 when he was 11 years old.

Beyond Type 1. “WHAT IS TYPE 1 DIABETES?” Online video clip. YouTube. June 29, 2018. Web.

Diabetes

Diabetes is a group of diseases that result in high blood glucose (sugar) levels. Cells in the body need glucose to function. Insulin, a hormone produced in the pancreas, lets the glucose enter the cells. In a person with diabetes, either not enough insulin is produced or the insulin does not work very well (this is called insulin resistance).

Approximately 90% of those with diabetes have type 2 diabetes. The rest have type 1 diabetes or diabetes due to other causes that also affect the person's ability to digest food, sometimes called type 3c diabetes. These other types of diabetes are not caused by a person's activity level, weight, or age.

Type 2 Diabetes

You probably already know a lot about type 2 diabetes. This is the "diabetes" shown on television ads and in most medical pamphlets. Sometimes the information from these sources can be a bit misleading, though. For example, there are many subtypes of type 2 diabetes, and some of them have little connection to obesity and lifestyle choices, either.

Type 2 diabetes occurs when a person's pancreas can't produce enough insulin. In fact, their pancreas may be producing much more insulin than a person without diabetes needs, but their body is not able to use the insulin properly. Not being able to use insulin properly is called insulin resistance. Belly fat is a source of insulin resistance, which is why losing weight can reverse type 2 diabetes.

As type 2 diabetes progresses, the high blood sugars damage the pancreas's ability to produce insulin. So, a person with type 2 diabetes may need to take insulin even if they are able to lower their insulin resistance.

Type 2 diabetes typically develops gradually. The first step in managing it is usually through diet changes to help the person lose fat tissue. The person may be prescribed pills or injectable medications that lower insulin resistance and hopefully enable the body to effectively use the insulin the pancreas produces. The final step is to prescribe insulin, to supplement what the pancreas produces.

Key Take-aways

  • There are many different kinds of diabetes, but the comments and concerns we hear about "diabetes" usually refer to type 2 diabetes.
  • Sometimes, type 2 diabetes is due to lifestyle choices such as obesity and inactivity.

Type 1 Diabetes: An Autoimmune Disease

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune disease. In autoimmune diseases, the body's immune system mistakenly identifies a healthy part of the body as a foreign invader and destroys it! In the case of type 1 diabetes, the body attacks and destroys the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas.

Phoenix Children's Hospital. “PCH Type 1 Children - What is Diabetes” Online video clip. YouTube. April 25, 2018. Web.

Autoimmune diseases have a genetic component and an environmental trigger.

For type 1 diabetes, the autoimmune attack is gradual. Usually, it takes several years for it to progress from antibodies in the bloodstream to elevated blood sugars. However, once blood sugars begin to elevate, it's important to act fast! The person can go into diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) quickly. And DKA can be deadly (we'll talk more about DKA in Section 5. High and Low Blood Sugars) In fact, the speed of the disease progression is one sign that a person has type 1 diabetes and not type 2.

Type 1 diabetes can be diagnosed at any age after about 6 months. The younger a person is when diagnosed, the stronger and faster the autoimmune attack tends to be. The most common age for people to be diagnosed with type 1 diabetes is around 14 years old.

Fluctuations in the incidence of type 1 diabetes in the United States from 2001 to 2015: a longitudinal study. BMC medicine, 15(1), 199. / CC BY 4.0

Type 1 diabetes used to be called "juvenile diabetes" because it is often diagnosed in childhood. However, the disease is chronic. It doesn't go away. It doesn't get easier. It doesn't change into a different form of diabetes. Although a person may be diagnosed with type 1 diabetes as a child, they live most of their lives with the disease as an adult.

A challenge those who develop the disease as adults face is getting the correct diagnosis. Those diagnosed as adults often have a slower-progressing form of the disease called latent autoimmune diabetes in adults (LADA) A study in Great Britain found that approximately one-third of those who develop type 1 diabetes as adults aged 30 or older are initially misdiagnosed as having type 2 diabetes. They are often initially treated only with medications that lower insulin resistance. Half of those who were misdiagnosed were still misdiagnosed 13 years later5. These misdiagnoses can result in years of high blood sugars and damage that leads to unnecessary diabetic complications.

Key Take-aways

  • Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune disease. This means it has a genetic component and an environmental trigger.
  • Type 1 diabetes can affect anyone, no matter how healthy they are or how old they are.

Who is at risk of developing Type 1 Diabetes?

Type 1 diabetes is rare. The risk of developing type 1 diabetes is about 0.3%.

Recall that autoimmune diseases have a genetic component and an environmental trigger. A person with the genes associated with type 1 diabetes has less than a 10% chance of actually developing the disease.

In type 1 diabetes, the genetic component is a marker on chromosome 6. The markers associated with type 1 diabetes are more common among Caucasians than those of African, Chinese, or South American descent.1

We think the trigger is often a viral illness. Viruses that may trigger type 1 diabetes include German measles, coxsackie, and mumps. People are more likely to contract viral illnesses when it's cold outside. Data show that those who live in northern climates are more likely to develop type 1 diabetes than those who live in southern climates, and more cases of type 1 diabetes are diagnosed in winter than in summer.

Family History of Type 1 Diabetes

A person is more likely to develop type 1 diabetes if there is a family history of type 1 diabetes. A study of children diagnosed with type 1 diabetes found about 30% had someone with type 1 diabetes in their extended family2. Data for the US show that about 0.3% of the population has type 1 diabetes, but those with a first-degree relative have about a 5% risk of developing it3.

Family history of autoimmune diseases

Autoimmune diseases tend to cluster, both in individuals and in families. Type 1 diabetes is not common, and many people with type 1 diabetes are the only ones in their family with the disease. However, their family members often have other autoimmune diseases. A study of children diagnosed with type 1 diabetes found about 22% had an immediate family member with an autoimmune disease (vs. 12.9% in those without type 1 diabetes) and 72% had someone with an autoimmune disease in their extended family (vs. 62% in those without type 1 diabetes)2.

Autoimmune diseases that may be present in family members of those with type 1 diabetes include the following:5

  • Addison's disease (also known as adrenal insufficiency)
  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis
  • Graves' disease
  • ankylosing spondylitis
  • rheumatoid arthritis
  • celiac disease
  • multiple sclerosis
  • pernicious anemia
  • primary biliary cirrhosis
  • psoriasis
  • systemic lupus erythematosus
  • vitiligo
  • Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (also known as GPA or Wegener's granulomatosis)

Key Take-aways

  • Type 1 diabetes is rare. Having the "right" genes for type 1 diabetes doesn't mean a person will develop the disease.
  • People with type 1 diabetes often have relatives with type 1 diabetes or other autoimmune diseases.

Try a few questions.

You’re waiting at a bus stop with Joshua, who is 55 years old and about 50 pounds (about 23 kg) overweight. As you chat, you learn his family doctor recently diagnosed him with diabetes based on bloodwork done at his last physical. He has started on Metformin (pills), but they don’t seem to be helping his blood sugar levels much, and he’s really tired, so he doesn’t exercise much. He’s also been busy lately helping out his sister, who has lupus.

a. You realize he was likely diagnosed with Type 2 Diabetes. Why?

Correct! Joshua has a lot of risk factors related to type 2 diabetes; of those listed, only the sister with lupus does not apply.

Try again.

Incorrect. Joshua has a lot of risk factors related to type 2 diabetes; of those listed, only the sister with lupus does not apply.

b. Is it worth suggesting he request more bloodwork to confirm he does not have type 1 diabetes?

Correct! Option B is better. Joshua does have a lot of risk factors related to type 2 diabetes. However, since he also has the family history of autoimmune disease (i.e., lupus), it is not unreasonable for him to request additional testing to confirm there are no antibodies present. Type 1 diabetes would need to be managed more aggressively, and it could take years for him and his doctors to sort out a misdiagnosis. In the meantime, his health could suffer and he might develop diabetic complications.

Personalize It

Think about your family history.

  • Do you have any relatives with an autoimmune disease?
  • Can you recall the signs and symptoms of diabetes:
    • excessive thirst
    • frequent urination
    • extreme hunger
    • weight loss
    • fatigue
    • irritability or other behaviour changes
    • fruity-smelling breath
    • blurred vision
    • yeast infection
