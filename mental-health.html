2. Type 1 Diabetes:
Mental Health

Objectives

This topic will demonstrate how to:

  • Respect the additional challenges and responsibilities a person with type 1 diabetes experiences.
  • Determine an appropriate balance between the desire to help, protect, or advise and respect for the person's autonomy to make their own decisions.
  • Help in ways that will be appreciated.

Introduction

Managing type 1 diabetes is all about numbers and measurements and preparing for the unexpected. It involves constant thought about:

  • The carbohydrate (and protein and fat) content of foods, and the corresponding insulin-to-carb (or fat or protein) ratios, correction factors
  • The timing of insulin injections, whether because the person's on a strict insulin regimen or because they want to avoid blood sugar spikes when eating
  • A1c results
  • Time-in-range
  • Others

Following predictable routines can help, but it's a disease that keeps changing. And it can be exhausting to effectively analyze all the data that is generated.

Type 1 diabetes is unique in that the patient doesn't simply follow the doctor's orders and take the prescribed amount of insulin every day until the doctor makes the next adjustment. It is the person with type 1 diabetes who decides how much insulin (a drug that is both life-saving and deadly) to take every time they inject. And the appropriate dose depends on so many factors: what they plan to eat, how active they've been, if they're ill or taking other drugs like steroids, if they are stressed, etc. Their decisions are guided by their health team, but the day-to-day decisions are theirs alone.

Mental Burden of Type 1 Diabetes

Living with type 1 diabetes can be

  • overwhelming: normal life activities have to be planned, and there are so many things to measure, record, and evaluate.
  • complex: many factors have to be considered even to determine an appropriate dose of insulin to take with a meal.
  • constant: it requires attention at least every few hours, regardless of what else is happening.
  • unforgiving: the risk of complications never goes away, and complications or other bad things may happen despite the person trying hard to self-manage the disease.
  • uncertain: it's often unclear whether a symptom is related to diabetes or diabetic complications or another problem, and this uncertainty can breed worry and anxiety.

Tamyka

"I had an overnight in the ICU after having DKA symptoms on and off for a few weeks. I tried to pull out of it, staying super hydrated, eating bananas, taking my multivitamins, checking my ketones, etc. Unfortunately, things out of my control like work and general life stressors really kicked in, and I went into DKA. I got through the physical aspects of it OK. I mean, it wasn't pleasant, but I can tolerate a lot of physical pain by this point. And the staff at the hospital were fantastic: they had positive attitudes, they were joking with me, and most importantly, I felt like they listened to me and respected my knowledge of my body and this disease. But now I'm really struggling with the mental and emotional tsunami that comes after:

  • The fear. Thinking about my mortality and all the what ifs. One of the doctors asked me if I had a DNR, and it really freaked me out. Not that I haven't thought about it, but being confronted with the question in real life made me question everything.
  • The shame. I drove myself to the ER. I didn't want to bother anyone or be a burden or make them wait in that awful ER waiting room. I only told my boss because I was supposed to work the next day, and my roommates so they would feed my pets. I still haven't told my parents because I have felt so judged in the past. I don't want to tell my friends ... I'm not sure why.
  • The anger that I couldn't control it.
  • The exhaustion.

I just want a body that functions properly."

Depression and Diabetes Distress1

People with type 1 diabetes have a greater risk of developing depression than those without a chronic illness. But they're also likely to develop something similar: diabetes distress. Diabetes distress is related to depression, but is measured using different scales:

  • emotional burden (similar to depression)
  • regimen distress
  • physician-related distress
  • interpersonal distress

Interestingly, when depression is detected and treated in a person with type 1 diabetes, their depression improves, but not their diabetes control. When diabetes distress is detected and treated, the distress improves and so does the diabetes control.

Diabetes Burnout

The day-to-day effort required to manage diabetes can be hard and frustrating, especially when the results don't match the effort the person is putting into managing the disease. At some stage, most people with type 1 diabetes experience worries, fear, and negative feelings. This can lead to diabetes burnout.

Burnout is more than feeling a little down. It includes overwhelming feelings of helplessness and hopelessness. It is one of the major psychological complications of diabetes. Signs include:

  • not checking blood sugar levels
  • stopping or reducing insulin injections
  • not caring about eating habits or exercise
  • trying to ignore or forget that they have diabetes

Ways for managing the emotional side of diabetes include the following:

  • Rewarding good behaviours, like checking blood sugar levels more often, regardless of what number shows up on the meter.
  • Talking about blood sugar numbers as "high" and "low" and NOT "good" or "bad", and framing these numbers as information to be used rather than as reflections on the person.

Tamyka

"Any time someone mentions possible complications to me, I shut down and completely refuse to do anything. Although the person is obviously just trying to convince me to take care of myself, I see it as a reminder that I'm different from everyone else. Why should I even bother trying? Why should I give myself all these needles that cause me pain if I'm just going to end up with these complications anyways? Might as well enjoy my life and act like I'm somewhat normal and die young."

Diabulimia and Disordered Eating

Unfortunately, a higher than normal proportion of those with type 1 diabetes develop eating disorders. Conservative estimates put the rate at about double the normal rate of eating disorders. The BBC documentary XXX claimed that 60% of all women with type 1 diabetes have experienced it.

Tamyka

"It often feels like food is my enemy. I was diagnosed a long time ago, when type 1 diabetics were put on a pretty stringent eating regime. There's a reason they discovered an increase in young type 1 diabetic girls developing eating disorders. At the time, I remember setting up my meal plan with my dietitian and talking about "exchanges" (fats, proteins, or starches), and just wanting her permission (or anyone's!) to EAT food. I just felt like it was better to have fewer exchanges on my meal plan. I'm guessing that feeling came from the pressure to keep everything (sugars, weight, and therefore, food) LOWER."

One particular form of disordered eating unique to those with type 1 diabetes is called diabulimia. It involves not covering food with enough (or any) insulin. This means the body cannot use all the glucose it takes in (in the form of food), and the kidneys do as much as they can to flush the unused glucose out in the urine. The person with diabetes loses weight, but experiences high blood glucose levels which can lead to DKA or diabetic complications.

Rachel

"I pretty much eat everything or nothing at all. I used to not take my short-acting insulin at all, or I'd make myself vomit so I could eat something but not need to take insulin for it. It's not something I talk about."

For more information about diabulimia and the relationships between type 1 diabetes and disordered eating, see the National Eating Disorders Association: Diabulimia

If you suspect a loved one has diabulimia or another eating disorder and type 1 diabetes, contact Diabulimia Helpline

Try a few questions.

Dave is at a Halloween party with his cousin Tim. Tim has type 1 diabetes, and he had a scary hypoglycemic episode about a month ago. Dave sees Tim grab a candy bar treat. Dave considers the following options. Which would be the best choice?

Correct! Option C is best. Tim is an adult who is responsible for his own decisions. He is not making a healthy choice, but neither is anyone else eating treats at the party. As long as Tim accurately tracks how many carbs he eats and gives an appropriate amount of insulin to cover it, he'll be ok, from a blood sugar standpoint. Neither taking away Tim's treat nor second-guessing his decision would help Tim achieve better control over his blood sugars; instead, these actions would build resentment and create distance between Tim and Dave.

Personalize It

Talk to the person you know with type 1 diabetes.

Find out what kind of diet and/or diet strategies they use to manage their diabetes.

  • How important are food labels?
  • What nutritional information do they look for?
  • What concerns do they have around eating, especially in a situation like a potluck or hosted dinner?
  • What frustrations have they experienced in the past?
  • What would help make them feel more at ease?
