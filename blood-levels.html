Type 1 Diabetes:
5. Emergencies

"Juice Boxes January 17, 201011" by Steven Depolo / CC BY 2.0

Objectives

After completing this topic, you will:

  • Recognize when a person with type 1 diabetes is likely to have high and low blood sugars, and the symptoms they may exhibit.
  • Know how to assist a person who is experiencing a diabetic emergency
  • Know when and how to administer glucagon.

Introduction

In a normal person, blood sugar levels tend to be a constant 5.0 mmol/L. They may rise slightly after a high-carb meal, or drop after exercise, but the body is usually able to quickly get the sugar levels back to a steady 5.0 mmol/L or so. A person with type 1 diabetes has to perform the role of a healthy pancreas. They try to time insulin and food so that they reach the bloodstream at the same time, giving that straight line. Often, however, their attempts look more like a roller-coaster track, soaring into the teens or higher, then dipping down into the threes or even twos, and then soaring again. The specific blood glucose range they try to stay within varies from person to person, but is usually something similar to staying in the range of 4–10 mmol/L.

What causes high or low blood sugar?

There are many things that affect whether a blood sugar value stays steady, increases, or decreases.

High blood sugar may be caused by:

  • Being less physically active than usual
  • Eating too soon after injecting insulin
  • Eating more than expected
  • Taking too little insulin
  • Being ill
  • Taking steroids
  • Being stressed
  • Hormonal changes

Low blood sugar may be caused by:

  • Being more physically active than usual
  • Not eating soon enough after injecting insulin
  • Not eating as much as expected
  • Taking too much insulin
  • Drinking alcohol
  • Being stressed

Key Take-aways

  • Many things can raise or lower blood sugar. Some can be planned for. Others cannot.

What are the signs of high or low blood sugar?

You may be able to recognize whether a person with type 1 diabetes has high or low blood sugar based on how they behave.

A person with high blood sugar may

  • be thirsty
  • urinate more often than usual, especially at night
  • be tired
  • have fruity-smelling breath

Very high blood sugar can make the person go into DKA. The person may experience

  • extreme thirst
  • frequent urination
  • high levels of ketones in the urine
  • nausea or vomiting
  • abdominal pain
  • confusion
  • a flushed face
  • rapid breathing
  • dry mouth and skin

A person with low blood sugar may

  • feel shaky, light-headed, or nauseated
  • act nervous, irritable, anxious
  • act confused, unable to concentrate
  • be hungry
  • have a fast heart rate
  • feel weak, drowsy
  • have numb or tingly lips

Low blood sugar can happen quickly, so it is important to treat it right away. If the person's blood sugar drops very low, they may need help from another person. Very low blood sugar can make the person:

  • feel confused and disoriented; they may react violently
  • lose consciousness
  • have a seizure

"I had a really bad low a few years ago. I was incoherent — it felt like my mouth was stuck in first gear. I was so hot and sweaty I ripped off all my clothes and started running naked around my house. I ended up running around in my front yard. My girlfriend was on the phone with 911, and because I was getting pretty aggressive, police, fire, and paramedics all showed up. It was quite the party."

Key Take-aways

  • High or low blood sugars can impair a person's judgement. They may say or do things that are out of character.

What should be done for a person with high or low blood sugar?

High Blood Sugar

A concern with high blood sugars is developing diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). DKA occurs when the body does not get enough insulin, and it can be deadly. Because DKA develops due to lack of insulin, it is possible for a person to develop it even with normal blood sugar levels, for example, if they are sick and haven't taken any insulin because they've been vomiting and didn't want to have low blood sugars.

A person with high blood sugar may need water or a sport drink with electrolytes to help flush the glucose out of their body and restore the appropriate electrolyte balance. They may avoid food or only eat low-carb foods such as green vegetables, eggs, meat, and cheese. Encourage them to take insulin to avoid going into diabetic ketoacidosis.

A person in DKA needs to go to the hospital for proper treatment. This is not a situation that can be fixed at home. It requires carefully reintroducing insulin and the appropriate electrolytes, and typically requires spending time in the ICU. The longer a person in DKA delays in going to the hospital for treatment, the worse their outcomes are likely to be.

Low Blood Sugar

A concern with low blood sugar is going into a diabetic coma.

A person with low blood sugar needs rapid-acting glucose to raise their blood sugar levels quickly. An appropriate correction dose is about 15 g of carbohydrates (e.g., 15 skittles or 4 glucose tablets or a small juice box). Having an additional follow-up snack with protein (e.g., peanut butter) is a good idea because it will help keep the blood sugar level steady.

A person with low blood sugar who is unconscious or who is dropping faster than they can treat on their own may need glucagon. See the next section for information about how to administer glucagon.

Tudor

"I like to eat chocolate candy when I'm low. I'm mean and combative and don't want juice. I know juice works faster, but at those times, I am irrational. Just give me what I want, and we can negotiate the juice after! Hopefully that little bit of chocolate will raise my sugar enough or put me in a more agreeable mood so I'll accept the juice."

One problem people with type 1 diabetes encounter is that treating low blood sugar is done simply with food or drink. Sometimes it can be hard for others to realize that a packet of skittles isn't just a snack, and juice boxes are used for treating low blood sugars and not as a cola alternative.

Sam

I'm getting fed up with a couple of roommates. They always eat or drink my low treatments and leave me with hardly any left, and they don't even let me know if I'm running low. When I mentioned I only had one juice box left today, one of them responded that it was no big deal—they'll get more tomorrow. That won't help me if I go low more than once tonight!

What about if you know something is wrong, but you aren't sure what to do?

  • Never give the person insulin. If their blood sugar is low, you could kill them. If their blood sugar is so high that they are not conscious, they need trained medical assistance. This is not something that can be treated at home.
  • It's always better to assume the person has low blood sugar and bring them something sugary to eat or drink. If their blood sugar is high, the little bit of sugar used to correct a low won't make a big difference. If they're low, the sugar can save their life.

Paige

I went out for dinner and drinks with my brother and a friend. We had the drinks first because we were still full from a really late lunch, and the alcohol hit me fast. My insulin pump got knocked off and my friend and brother freaked out, and my friend called 911. There was no issue … my blood sugar was in range, but they didn't know that. Well, as my friend was calling 911, another person out with us decided I needed insulin and injected me with 100 units! That's more than I take for 3 days! My blood sugar dropped dangerously low in less than 5 minutes, and I passed out due to low blood sugar. EMS did all they could to get me back. Three bags of water plus 5 sugar shots later (I know this from what the doctor told me), I now have to stay in the ICU for 3 days because they have to get this all out of me. Who just gives someone a shot of insulin!? That person almost killed me.

Key Take-aways

  • Never inject a person with insulin.
  • When in doubt, assume a person with type 1 diabetes is experiencing low blood sugar and bring them something sweet (NOT diet), like a juice box.
  • Print this Diabetic Emergency Aid (PDF) and post it somewhere noticeable to help you recognize the signs of a diabetic emergency and know to do.

Giving glucagon for low blood sugars

If a person experiencing low blood sugar is unable to eat or drink, they may need a glucagon injection. Glucagon tells the liver to release some of the glucose it has stored into the bloodstream. Note that glucagon does NOT work if a person has had something to drink recently. The liver is busy dealing with the alcohol and won't respond to the glucagon.

Note: Before giving a person an injection of glucagon, make sure you have their permission to use it on them in an emergency.

Watch this video to see how to give a glucagon injection (this video was designed for parents caring for their children, but applies to adults as well, following the directions for a child over 12 years of age).

University College London Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust. “How to do a glucagon injection.” Online video clip. YouTube. May 26, 2015. Web.

Health Canada recently approved a nasal glucagon kit. The glucagon is delivered through a nasal spray instead of requiring an injection.

Key Take-aways

  • Before giving a person an glucagon injection, make sure you have their permission.
  • Glucagon is ineffective if the person has been drinking.
