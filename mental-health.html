2. Type 1 Diabetes:
Mental Health

"Enough Insulin! Cure Diabetes Already!" by Jeff Fillmore / CC BY-NC-SA 2.0

Objectives

After completing this topic, you will be able to:

  • Respect the extra challenges and responsibilities a person with type 1 diabetes experiences, particularly emotional and mental challenges.
  • Determine an appropriate balance between desiring to help, protect, or advise and respecting the person's autonomy to make their own decisions.
  • Help in ways that will be appreciated.

Introduction

Managing type 1 diabetes is all about numbers and measurements and timing and preparing for the unexpected.

Managing type 1 diabetes is unique in that the patient doesn't simply follow the doctor's orders and take a prescribed amount of insulin every day until the doctor makes the next change. Instead, it is the person with type 1 diabetes who decides how much insulin (a drug that is both life-saving and deadly) to take every time they inject. And the appropriate dose depends on so many factors: what they plan to eat, how active they've been, if they're ill or taking other drugs like steroids, if they are stressed, etc. They follow guidelines provided by their health team, but the day-to-day decisions are theirs alone.

Mental Burden of Type 1 Diabetes

Living with type 1 diabetes can be

  • overwhelming: normal life activities have to be planned, and there are so many things to measure, record, and analyze.
  • complex: many factors have to be considered even to determine an appropriate dose of insulin to take with a meal.
  • constant: a person with type 1 diabetes makes about 180 health-related decisions each day.
  • unforgiving: the risk of complications never goes away, and bad things may happen despite the person's best efforts to manage the disease.
  • uncertain: it's often unclear whether a symptom is related to diabetes or diabetic complications or another problem, and this uncertainty can breed worry and anxiety.

People have different priorities. Achieving good clinical outcomes is not always at the top of the list. Some want good control over blood sugar levels to increase their lifespan and support better physical health; others want to be more free of the daily diabetes burden and choose to focus less meticulously on the daily management.

Key Take-aways

  • Diabetes is a difficult disease to manage.

Emotional Burden of Type 1 Diabetes

Besides managing all the data that they generate, people with type 1 diabetes have to manage the emotional toll that comes with having a chronic illness.

Erica

"I was recently diagnosed with type 1 diabetes. Food is so social. And I feel like I can't be spontaneous with my diet anymore or eat out as easily. There is more emotion around food now, too. Less joy."

Melanie

"I had an overnight in the ICU after having DKA symptoms on and off for a few weeks. I tried to pull out of it, staying super hydrated, eating bananas, taking my multivitamins, checking my ketones, etc. Unfortunately, things out of my control like work and general life stressors kicked in, and I went into DKA. I got through the physical aspects of it OK. I mean, it wasn't pleasant, but I can tolerate a lot of physical pain by this point. The staff at the hospital were fantastic: they had positive attitudes and, most importantly, I felt like they listened to me and respected my knowledge of my body and this disease. But now I'm struggling with the mental and emotional feelings that come after:

  • The fear. Thinking about my mortality and all the what ifs. One of the doctors asked me if I had a DNR, and it freaked me out.
  • The shame. I drove myself to the ER. I didn't want to bother anyone or be a burden or make them wait in that awful ER waiting room. I only told my boss because I was supposed to work the next day, and my roommates so they would feed my pets. I still haven't told my parents because I have felt so judged in the past. I don't want to tell my friends, either.
  • The anger that I couldn't control it.
  • The exhaustion.

I just want a body that functions properly."

Other people's responses

A big part of the emotional burden is dealing with the responses of others towards type 1 diabetes. Other people's negative or unexpected responses can make people with type 1 diabetes feel emotionally vulnerable, especially when the responses exhibit misconceptions and lack of understanding, particularly about the differences between type 1 and type 2 diabetes. Confusing the two types of diabetes has a major emotional impact on those with type 1 diabetes and causes feelings of frustration, anger, and a sense of not being understood or of being judged. Fortunately, meaningful relationships and good social support are stabilizing and help those with type 1 diabetes feel more in control1.

Kim

"A coworker who knows I have type 1 diabetes grabbed a candy bar out of my hand, threw it in the garbage, then chastised me for eating sugar. I was eating it because my blood sugar was low and she just threw away the very thing that was saving my life! I can't stand when someone judges (especially misjudges) a diabetic! I made her buy me another candy bar."

Key Take-aways

  • There is a lot of stigma around diabetes related to type 2 diabetes and its connection with obesity. And that stigma can feel doubly unfair when it is directed towards those with type 1 diabetes.

Depression and Diabetes Distress1

People with type 1 diabetes have a greater risk of developing depression than those without a chronic illness. But they're also likely to develop something similar: diabetes distress. Diabetes distress is related to depression, but is measured using different scales:

  • emotional burden (similar to depression)
  • regimen distress
  • physician-related distress
  • interpersonal distress

Interestingly, when depression is detected and treated in a person with type 1 diabetes, their depression improves, but not their diabetes control. When diabetes distress is detected and treated, the distress improves and so does the diabetes control.

Diabetes Burnout

The day-to-day effort required to manage diabetes can be hard and frustrating, especially when the results don't match the effort the person is putting into managing the disease. At some stage, most people with type 1 diabetes experience worries, fear, and negative feelings. This can lead to diabetes burnout.

Burnout is more than feeling a little down. It includes overwhelming feelings of helplessness and hopelessness. It is one of the major psychological complications of diabetes. Signs include:

  • not checking blood sugar levels
  • stopping or reducing insulin injections
  • not caring about eating habits or exercise
  • trying to ignore or forget that they have diabetes

Ways you can help a person manage the emotional side of diabetes include the following:

  • Empathize with how difficult it can be to attempt to recreate a bodily process manually and how that affects every aspect of daily life.
  • Reward good behaviours, like checking blood sugar levels more often, regardless of what number shows up on the meter.
  • Talk about blood sugar numbers as "high" and "low" and NOT "good" or "bad", and frame these numbers as information to be used rather than as judgements about the person.
  • Use other neutral words: "checking" rather than "testing" blood sugar levels; "management" rather than "control" of diabetes
  • Acknowledge they are unique and that strategies that work for others with type 1 diabetes may not work for them. Encourage them to experiment to figure out what works for them.
  • Unless the person has asked for you to step in and take care of diabetes tasks, let them take the lead in their care, even if it is challenging to watch them fail to lead at all.

Chloe

"Any time someone mentions possible complications to me, I shut down and completely refuse to do anything. Although the person is obviously just trying to convince me to take care of myself, I see it as a reminder that I'm different from everyone else. Why should I even bother trying? Why should I give myself all these needles that cause me pain if I'm going to end up with these complications anyways? Might as well enjoy my life and act like I'm somewhat normal and die young."

Key Take-aways

  • A person who is experiencing depression-like symptoms will likely benefit from seeing a therapist who specializes in chronic medical conditions so that both the non-diabetes issue and those related to diabetes can be discussed and improved.
  • Blame and judgement usually don't help. Instead, try rewarding small steps and trying to help the person not feel judged even if they haven't managed their diabetes well.

Fear of Low Blood Sugars

Extreme low blood sugars are scary. They can even be fatal, especially if they occur at night while the person is asleep. Because experiencing low blood sugar is unpleasant and can be embarrassing if it happens in public, those with type 1 diabetes try to avoid it.

Total avoidance is difficult, however. In one study, 36% of those with type 1 diabetes reported going into a coma due to low blood sugars at least once in their life. These extreme low blood sugars are often feared as much as diabetic complications. Some people with type 1 diabetes fear low blood sugars so much they deliberately keep their blood sugars higher than desirable, risking the long-term consequences of diabetic complications.

Iga

"I went low at work today after a lunch with my team. By the time we got back to the office, my sugar level was really low even though I had just eaten. I had one juice box, but needed more, and I started to lose control of my hands and couldn't get the next box open. I asked for some help with my juice box and my coworker called 911. Soon, there were 5 people in my cube plus paramedics and a stretcher to take me to the hospital. By this point, my blood sugar was back to normal, so I signed off to deny transportation to the hospital. But while all this was going on, I started crying and my muscles started jerking around and my co-workers got to see first-hand how horrid this disease is. My boss sent me home, but wouldn't let me drive, so I had to wait for my husband to come pick me up. Now I'm super embarrassed. How am I supposed to go back and face these people?"

Key Take-aways

  • Low blood sugar is frightening, and can cause embarrassing emotional and physical responses.

Diabulimia and Disordered Eating

Unfortunately, a higher than normal proportion of those with type 1 diabetes develop eating disorders. Women with type 1 diabetes are two times more likely to have an eating disorder than woman without diabetes. Eating disorders occur in about 31-40% of women with type 1 diabetes aged 15 to 30. In fact, the BBC documentary Diabulimia: The World's Most Dangerous Eating Disorder claimed that 60% of all women with type 1 diabetes have experienced it.

Maya

"It often feels like food is my enemy. I was diagnosed a long time ago, when type 1 diabetics were put on a pretty stringent eating regime. There's a reason they discovered an increase in young type 1 diabetic girls developing eating disorders. At the time, I remember setting up my meal plan with my dietitian and talking about "exchanges" (fats, proteins, or starches), and just wanting her permission (or anyone's!) to EAT food. I just felt like it was better to have fewer exchanges on my meal plan. I'm guessing that feeling came from the pressure to keep everything (sugars, weight, and therefore, food) LOWER."

One particular form of disordered eating unique to those with type 1 diabetes is called diabulimia. It involves not covering food with enough (or any) insulin. This means the body cannot use all the glucose it takes in (in the form of food), and the kidneys do as much as they can to flush the unused glucose out in the urine. The person with diabetes loses weight, but experiences high blood glucose levels which can lead to DKA or diabetic complications.

Rachel

"I pretty much eat everything or nothing at all. I used to not take my short-acting insulin at all, or I'd make myself vomit so I could eat something but not need to take insulin for it. It's not something I talk about."

Dwayne

"Diabulimia. I used to suffer with it and didn't realize it. Never injecting, or injecting negligible amounts. Eating high-sugar, fatty foods. Never putting weight on. Always peeing and sweating. Only when I started taking the right amount of insulin for my food and carb counting properly did I put weight on. A downside is that once you've done it and realized how easy it is to lose weight, it's very tempting to go back there if you want to lose weight. It's something I've considered many, many times. It's a demon in and of itself."

For more information about diabulimia and the relationships between type 1 diabetes and disordered eating, see the National Eating Disorders Association: Diabulimia

If you suspect a loved one has diabulimia or another eating disorder and type 1 diabetes, contact Diabulimia Helpline

Key Take-aways

  • Women with type 1 diabetes are at high risk of developing disordered eating at some point.
  • If you suspect that the person you know with type 1 diabetes, male or female, has developed an eating disorder, contact Diabulimia Helpline

Correct Response

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune disease that has nothing to do with a person's appearance (and, by extension, diet or lifestyle choices). It is often an invisible disease, and a person with type 1 diabetes may not look noticeably different from others.

Personalize It

Talk to the person you know with type 1 diabetes.

Find out what parts of having diabetes are the most challenging for them to manage.

  • How does diabetes affect them emotionally?
  • When or how often do they struggle with it being overwhelming, complex, constant, unforgiving, or uncertain?
  • How open are they about sharing that they have type 1 diabetes with others? Have they ever had a negative experience? What happened?
